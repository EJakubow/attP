Antibiotic resistance is disseminating into a global crisis. Bacteria are developing immunity to most everyday drugs. Overprescribing such drugs, along with a lack of development of new drugs, has set the stage where superbugs are now a commonplace occurrence. Consequently, a significant amount of research has been redirected to tackle this rising problem.

Nature offers a natural response to bacteria â€“ Bacteriophages (phages). Phages, aka bacteria-eaters, infect and destroy bacteria cells. But there is one caveat. These phages are very specific to hosts. Hence, host ranges are not easily identifiable. Multiple avenues of explorations, such as viral tagging, wet lab research, and computational methods have been undertaken to assist with phage-host identification. Phage Host Finder aims to assist in the research by providing a resource to predict phage-host interactions more efficiently. This site focuses on a unique feature, the attP/attB sites, a homologous sequence present in both temperate phage and bacteria respectively. These sites are the attachment sites where the phage integrates its DNA into the bacteria genome and are frequently found adjacent to the integrase gene in the phage genome and within or near a tRNA gene in the bacterial genome. A match between phage and bacteria would indicate the likelihood of a successful phage infection of that host.

Input Format:
Phage Host finder currently accepts only accession numbers for nucleotide sequences as inputs. Future updates will include additional options.

Output Format: The results will return a table listing the families in the Actinobacteria phylum that the attP/attB sequence was located in, along with the total number of hits within the families. The more hits within a family, the stronger the likelihood of a familial host match. The results will also include a consensus sequence to highlight the most common nucleotide at each position.

If no results are returned, it can be attributed to a few possibilities. This site is currently in its first phase of development and is limited in scope. If the phage hasn't been annotated yet or the attP/attB recognition sequence lies further away from the integrase gene, this site currently does not have the capabilities of recognizing those variations. Another possibility to consider is that the phage may not be temperate one, and therefore doesn't possess this mechanism of integration.
